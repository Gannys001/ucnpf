John Gallagher: Rethinking the Strategy of Counterterrorism of the United States

 

1970-01-01 08:00:00  



 

The Middle East has long suffered from ineffective governance that marginalizes and persecutes some of its citizens, while favoring others based on religious, sectarian, and ethnic backgrounds as a way to maintain control.  This kind of predatory governance is inherently unstable and creates conditions conducive to upheaval and the spread of violent extremists that kill in the name of “God.”  It is also important to note that the historic competition between Sunni and Shia has been described appropriately as a “Cold War between Saudi Arabia and Iran” played out with violence in the weak states between them.  Further, the past decade has seen an information revolution and a diffusion of power away from state control – where people groups (including some terror groups) communicate, organize, and travel within and across borders much more freely.  This collective action – combined with generations of frustration and anti-government sentiment in the region – triggered the “Arab Awakening” of 2010.  Some states in the region responded to the historic unrest with attempts at reform (e.g., Tunisia, Yemen), some with a mix of reform and suppression (e.g., Bahrain, Egypt), and some with a heavy “crack-down” leading to widespread conflict between loyalists and the opposition (e.g., Libya, Syria).  In Syria, the result is millions displaced, hundreds of thousands killed and held captive for their beliefs and/or gender, even more living in fear of ISIS’ and Assad’s brutality, and sympathy for ISIS’ worldview inspiring extremists around the world to pledge allegiance and carry out attacks well beyond the region.  

 

5, Further, most scholars would agree that the U.S.'s policy to fight with ISIS has been failed. What is your thinking in the battle with ISIS?  

 

The battle with ISIS is civilization’s battle, and nations that do not recognize this will eventually be faced with the violent extremist threat in various forms inside its borders. Waiting too long means ISIS and its worldview will only be more entrenched and will require greater resources and time to diminish.  By its nature, when not directly pressured, ISIS tries to form a state and organize its fighting forces like a military.  When pressured (as is happening now in Mosul) ISIS will dissipate and act more like an insurgency with IEDs and hostages.  If pursued further, ISIS will blend into the population and migrate to other places of weak governance that may offer an advantage to try to reclaim ground.  The U.S. approach to ISIS aims to account for all of these dimensions, and has diminished the organization’s leadership, territory, freedom of maneuver, and financial resources.
