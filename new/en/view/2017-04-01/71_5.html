John Gallagher: Rethinking the Strategy of Counterterrorism of the United States

However, it is known that U.S. policy limits American presence on the ground, instead enabling greater action from Iraq and regional powers.  This will prolong the fight against ISIS.  While a U.S.-unilateral, military-heavy approach could succeed in overwhelming ISIS in a very short time – this is not a long-term solution to the cauldron of poor governance, violent extremism, and sectarian strife in the region.  Scholars who “agree” that U.S. policy has failed may be rendering judgment prematurely.  This is among the most complex threats in all of modern conflict, it began many decades before ISIS, and the ongoing refugee crisis is equally complex.  The bigger question is whether the U.S. is “in the arena” providing leadership and resources alongside other stakeholders to help solve this global problem.  That answer is “yes” regardless of who wins the presidency in November, because these issues demand enlightened leadership and the world’s full engagement, as fundamental questions of human identity and worth are at stake.

What is the implication of this country's increasing presence in the Middle East? 7, Additionally, the Obama administration has tried a new approach to Iran. They negotiated a nuclear deal together. What does all of this mean to the U.S.'s interest in this region?

 

It is an important question whether Russia could and would ever be a robust part of a region-wide effort to stabilize the Middle East, and similarly whether China could do the same.  Further, Iran has been given a rare opportunity through the recent nuclear deal with the U.S. (despite all of the deal’s shortcomings) to undertake bold reforms, eliminating its terrorism sponsorship and malign activities throughout the region which, if authentic and verifiable, could enable it to play a more constructive role in a wide-ranging effort to counter ISIS.  This is, of course, is unlikely given the areas of disagreement between the U.S., Russia, China, and Iran, but perhaps the emerging era of international relations (amidst rising global instability) will be defined by the concept of the “non-traditional ally” – where nations that have both converging and diverging interests work to establish common ground where their interests converge, and then use that common ground as a basis for dialogue on the diverging, more difficult issues.  Truly understanding the long term, destabilizing and global threat of violent extremism may just warrant this kind of unprecedented cooperation in response.  In contrast, as the U.S. and Russia array themselves on different sides of the Iraq-Syria crisis, the risk of misstep or provocation that could put the two nations in direct conflict with each other only increases.
